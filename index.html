The challenge

Why menstrual hygiene keeps girls out of school

Menstrual hygiene is a basic part of health, dignity and self-determination, a right every woman should have access to. Yet for many girls from low-income families in Tanzania, products are simply unavailable or unaffordable. The result: recurring absences that hold back their education and their future.

Menstruation is a natural process, but it is still surrounded by shame, taboo and a lack of education. Combined with poverty and missing infrastructure, this deepens the disadvantage girls already face.

up to 5school days a girl loses every single month during her period
  • ✕Missed lessons. Many girls skip school during their period; in some regions they are excluded from daily life altogether.
  • ✕Unhygienic substitutes. Unable to afford clean pads, girls resort to old rags or banana fibres.
  • ✕Nothing available. In rural areas especially, affordable hygiene products are almost impossible to find.
  • ✕Disposables don't work. Single-use pads are no sustainable solution for poor families, and harm the environment when not disposed of properly.

About AfriCraft

A fair-trade social enterprise, since 2004

AfriCraft empowers communities and young people through waste collection and recycling, promoting sustainable living and fair trade across Tanzania. What began as a small home workshop with two employees now works with over 200 artisans.

The same artisans who turn discarded materials into handcrafted products also produce the Binti Kits, connecting social impact with environmental responsibility. AfriCraft follows World Fair Trade Organization standards and pays fair wages.
